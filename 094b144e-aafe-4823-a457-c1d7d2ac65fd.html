While it is possible in a few cases, with great difficulty, to obtain spectra of the atmospheres of exoplanets, we do have a crude measure of planet composition for some dozens of exoplanets.  This comes from the density of the planet:  Density = Mass / Volume = Mass / (4/3 pi) Radius3 for a sphere.  A trivial example might be: a pound of feathers occupies a much larger volume than a pound of steel, so steel has a much higher density than feathers.  

When a planet is detected both using the radial velocity method (which gives a measurement of the mass) and the transit method (which gives a measurement of the radius), we can combine these to estimate the density.  A gas giant planet will have low density below 1 g/cm3 (one gram per cubic centimeter).  A planet of solid water ice will have density at 1 g/cm^3.  Solid rocky (silicate) planets should have densities around 3-4 g/cm3.   A pure iron planet would have density around 8 g/cm3.  So we have a real ability to evaluate something about the composition of planets without actually measuring their spectrum.  See the artist's drawing below for predictions of different planet compositions.

Jupiter of course lies at M=1.0 MJup and R=1.0 RJup.  Many of the exoplanets are roughly around Jupiter's position in the diagram, though with somewhat bigger radii.   This suggest they are gaseous hydrogen-rich giants.  Earth lies at M=0.003 MJup  and R=0.1 RJup at the bottom-left of the diagram, and a few Earth-like rocky planets have been found.  And of course, there are planets with intermediate masses and radii between Earth and Jupiter.  But perhaps the most interesting aspect is the wide range in radii for a fixed mass ... this suggests a variety of compositions are present.

It is easier to interpret these results from a similar plot of the lower-left portion of the diagram made last year by the Kepler Team:

Here we see that planets near the upper envelope of the distribution (higher radius for a given mass) have densities around 0.5-1 g/cm3, roughly similar to Saturn which is dominated by H and He (hydrogen and helium).  Those in the middle of the distribution have densities similar to Uranus & Neptune which are dominated by CH4 and NH3 (methane and ammonia).  But a few have densities similar to Earth (silicate rock with iron core) or even denser (pure iron?).  

Finally, I reproduce below a similar plot from (student) Lauren Weiss and (professor) Geoffroy Marcy at University of California - Berkeley, members of the Kepler Team.  You can find their paper here.   The dots represent the observed exoplanets (error bars omitted).  The dashed red curve shows a relationship expected for Earth-like composition (silicates plus an iron core); they believe this fits the data for planets with small radii, <1.5 REarth.  The dashed black line is fitted to the data for larger planets.  Here the mass rises much slower than (radius)3, indicating lower densities for larger planets.  The two blue dots at the upper right are Uranus and Neptune ... so it appears that most exoplanets with radii similar to these planets have lower mass, and hence lower densities, than Uranus & Neptune.  They claim their curves are evidence for a transition from rocky planets (r<1.5 REarth) to gaseous planets (r>1.5 REarth).  This interpretation is being hotly debated in the research community.

We immediately can make a few conclusions:  

  1. There are exoplanets that seem fairly similar -- in mass and radius -- to Earth, Uranus/Neptune and Saturn/Jupiter.  But there is a lot of scatter in this relationship suggesting that different planets have different compositions.  This suggests that both rocky and gaseous planets are common among exoplanets.  There may be a transition between the two types around radium ~1.5 REarth or  mass ~2 MEarth, but this isn't yet very clear.  
  2. Most of the planets follow a broad relationship where radius increases with mass.  This is expected for planets of any reasonable composition.  But it is hard to distinguish true differences in planet density from measurement errors from the noisy (both radial velocity and transit) data and complications in the models needed to estimate masses and radii.
