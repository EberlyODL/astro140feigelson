Having found protoplanetary disks orbiting virtually all young stars, we now have to figure out how planets form in these disks.  Planet formation is still incompletely understood ... many physical processes seem to be involved, and astrophysicists are struggling today to put them all together in a coherent fashion.  Much insight, however, is obtained by studying ancient bodies in our Solar System.  Earth and the other planets have erased their origins, but the small rocky bodies from the Asteroid Belt, a few of which have landed on Earth as meteorites, provide essential clues.  So we model the origin of our own Solar System, and adapt that model to explain exoplanetary systems.

Origin of our Solar System

During the 20th century, this view was elaborated, based on part on study of meteorites (rocks from the Asteroid Belt that have landed by chance on Earth) and interstellar dust.  Meteorites show dust and pebbles from the formation process, and interstellar dust shows spectral signatures of silicates (rock) and soot-like carbon.  These dust particles were ejected into the interstellar medium by red giant stars and collected in molecular clouds in the Galaxy.  When the conditions are right, portions of the cloud collapse due to self-gravity, and stars form (look again Matthew Bate's marvelous computer simulation of star formation in a cloud from Week 3).   Due to swirling motions (called `angular momentum' in physics), not all of the gas and dust can enter the protostar, but orbits the protostar in a protoplanetary disk.  

For our Solar System, conditions in the protoplanetary disk 4.5 billion years ago can not be directly inferred from the larger planets like Earth or Jupiter, but are revealed in smaller ancient bodies like asteroids (most in the Asteroid Belt between Mars and Jupiter, and in the Kuiper Belt beyond Neptune), comets, and tiny dust particles between these bodies.  

        

In this well-accepted planet formation model, dust particles in the disk around the proto-Sun stick together.  Some (for reasons not yet understood) are melted into small (millimeter-sized) chondrules seen in meteorites. You can see these melted chondrules in the photo above right.  Here an ancient meteorite (small asteroid that fell by chance on Earth and was retrieved by scientists) is cut open to show it is made mostly of millimeter-sized melted pieces of rock; these are the chondrules.  One of the problems in modern planet formation theory is we have several models for chondrule melting, but don't know which (if any) is right).

In the protoplanetary disk, the tiny interstellar dust particles and pebbles collide and merge, forming planetisimals on roughly kilometer scales. In the early stages, the merging is caused by sticking; this sticking probably most effective in the cooler parts of the disk outside the snow line (T=273C where water freezes). When the bodies become sufficient large (roughly kilometer size), then gravitational focusing becomes important.  The larger planetisimals grow faster, sweeping all of the rock in a ring around the Sun. After tens of millions of years, only a few large, well-separated protoplanets are left.  Other small bodies are scattered to outer eccentric orbits, as we see today in the Kuiper Belt and Oort Cloud where most comets reside.  The few very largest bodies attract hydrogen and other gas â€¦ these become the giant gas planets like Jupiter.  Others remain rocky like Earth and Mars, with a bit of atmosphere from the volatile molecules (like water, methane, ammonia, and carbon dioxide) that was originally in the interstellar dust.  This planet formation scenario is called the core accretion model.

New models of our early Solar System

In a wonderful series of papers in 2005, Alesandro Morbidelli, Hal Levison and colleagues elaborated on this model for our Solar System at later times (~50-1000 million years after formation).  Now called the `Nice model' after Nice Observatory in France, a sudden instability in the planets occurs: Neptune and Uranus can trade places, and a flood of planetisimals bombard the inner Solar System, causing the mare on our Moon.  

See Levison's simulations of Solar System dynamics according to the Nice model.  A more detailed description of the model is available here.  

Finally, we examine dynamical simulations of the early Solar System that follows water molecules.  In the inner Solar System, inside the `snow line' around 3-5 Astronomical Units, water is vaporized (steam) and cannot enter planetary bodies.  Outside the snow line, the water is solid (ice) and can be in growing planets.  So, it is a puzzle why Earth (and frozen in its polar ice caps, Mars) today has any water at all!! So there is a general problem in getting water on planets in the Habitable Zones.

The Earth and Mars must have inherited water from bodies that had been formed in the outer Solar Sytem, and later impacted Earth.  One possibility is that some of Earth's water comes from comets.  Another possibility is that is comes from planetisimals that bounced around in the early Solar System.  Above are snapshots from a simulation by Avi Mandell, Sean Raymond and Steinn Sigurdsson (Avi was a Penn State graduate student and Steinn is a Penn State faculty member) in 2007.  Here follow the blue representing water-rich bodies, and `HZ' represents the Habitable Zone.  If a Jupiter-like planet migrates inward, then water-rich bodies can also  migrate inwards and impact only an Earth-like planet in an Earth-like orbit.
