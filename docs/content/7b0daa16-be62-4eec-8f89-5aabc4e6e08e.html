Papers for ASTRO 140 Life in the Universe

GOALS

  • Provide students with the opportunity to investigate a narrow scientific issue in some detail;
  • Challenge the student to assimilate diverse information into a coherent narrative;
  • Provide training in the art of scientific writing.

TOPICS   Paper topics should be chosen from the list of recommended topics (with recommended research materials) provided by the instructor later in the semester.  A student with a strond desire for a special topic should present it, with ideas for research materials, for prior approval by the instructor.  Our textbook and Wikipedia provide useful starting points for your research on most topics, but are not enough by themselves for a strong paper.  If you have difficulty identifying resources (or obtaining full-text versions), please freely contact the instructor. 

LENGTH AND FORMAT   The typical length is 3-4 pages text followed by pages for references and useful figures (perhaps .jpg taken from the Web). A cover page can be added.  The paper should be submitted prepared with a word processing system like Microsoft Word or Google Docs. The text is single-spaced with Arial or Times Roman font, 12pt font size, with 1" margins for 8.5x11 inch or A4 paper.  Papers may be submitted in either .docx or .pdf fomat with file names like "Lastname_Paper1.pdf".    

RESEARCH MATERIAL AND REFERENCING Each paper should make significant use of at least three sources beyond the text and Wikipedia.  Sources can include both general background sources and specialized sources;  it is great to have one or more peer-review research journal articles.   All sources(except the text and Wikipedia) should be included in the list of references and cited in the text.  Avoid many similar citations to the same source.  The example here in red illustrates referencing of book, article and Web material:

Cyanobacteria, also called blue-green algae, is not commonly seen today but dominated all lifeforms before the Cambrian Explosion 0.5 billion years ago.  Through photosynthesis, it had a dramatic effect on the composition of the Earth's atmosphere around 2 billion years ago (Kasting 2005, Chpt. 4; Lane 2010).  American and Australian geobiologists recently traced the genetic family tree of cyanobacteria found that only one of dozens of species evolved to produce oxygen (Perkins 2017).

Kasting, J. 2010, How to Find a Habitable Earth, Princeton Univ Press

Lane, Nick 2010, "First breath: Earth's billion-year struggle for oxygen", New Scientist, February pp36–9

Perkins, R. 2017, "How do plants make oxygen? Ask cyanobacteria", press release,  http://www.caltech.edu/news/how-do-plants-make-oxygen-ask-cyanobacteria-54559

GRADING  The grade will be based foughly equally on (a) the knowledge and understanding of the issue under study, and (b) the clarity and quality of the communication (Science Writing below).  For Paper 1, students will submit a draft to the instructor for suggestions on how to improve the organization and writing.  It need not be complete but should have at least: a detailed outline of the paper structure, two or more fully written paragraphs, and research references for the full paper.   The instructor will then return it with comments and suggestions for improvement to help the student write a strong paper.  Paper II will be written by the student independently based on a different set of topics provided by the instructor.  Paper I draft is worth 2%, Paper I completed 10%, and Paper II 15% of the course grade. 

SCIENCE WRITING  Quality science writing is a skill well worth developing and exercising. The paper's overall organization looks like a U: it begins with a short statement of motivation or scientific issue; most of the text presents the evidence or argument (some combination of empirical findings and scientific modelling); and the paper ends with a discussion of how the results relate to the original motivation. Within this framework, the paragraphs should follow a logical trail. Each paragraph should communicate one thought clearly.  Within a paragraph, sentences should be well-constructed.  Nouns and verbs should be informative (e.g. "The Martian photographs show ..." rather than "It was found ...").  First person pronouns should be avoided (e.g. not "I" or "my").  Colloquialisms and cliches should be avoided (e.g. "The experiment failed" rather than "It was a dismal failure").  Please read the following background materia: 

  • Eric Feigelson's general advice for astronomy writing (download)
  • Caty Pilachowski's tips for astronomy writing
  • William Safire's rules for writers (humorous)
  • Writing in the sciences
  • Astro writing problems (Feigelson)

ACADEMIC INTEGRITY Plagiarism is strictly prohibited. See the University document https://psu.box.com/v/PlagiarismAndYou for more details.  The presence of plagiarized text will be checked using specialized University-provided software. For example, you are not permitted to cut and paste text from the Web.   Extensive quotations from any source are also inappropriate.  Rather, the student should seek to understand and restate the issues in a new voice, summarizing and synthesizing diverse material (with appropriate reference).
