Astronomers have made remarkable progress in discovering and characterizing exoplanets and the disks from which they emerged.  Astrophysicists are making significant progress in modeling the processes of planet formation and planetary system evolution, although major questions still remain.  Overall, from the 1980s to the present day, astronomy has laid an empirical foundation for the argument that the conditions for life are prevalent throughout the Galaxy.  This is a remarkable scientific achievement.   Here is an outline of what we have learned.

  • We now have direct observational evidence (IRAS, Hubble, Spitzer space telescopes) that most or all stars are born with protoplanetary disks arising from material that had too much spin to fall onto the protostar.  These disks have dust, molecular gases and ices inherited from the interstellar cloud, and these are very similar to the composition of ancient bodies (comets, asteroids) in our Solar System.
  • Astrophysicists have a model where the tiny rock+ice interstellar `dust' particles merge to form pebbles, then boulders, then planetesimals, and eventually protoplanets.  These models are validated by computer simulations, and match reasonably well to properties of meteorites (pieces of asteroids) found on Earth.  
  • Recent surveys of older stars using a variety of sensitive and specialized techniques (radial velocity, transit, microlensing, direct imaging) have found several thousand planets orbiting ordinary stars in our Galaxy.
  • Careful calculations based on the limitations of these surveys indicate that most, or all, stars have planetary systems ... thus most protoplanetary disks successfully produce planets.  And recent (2013-18) estimates indicate that hundreds of millions, or billions, of Earth-like planets in Habitable Zones probably exist in our Galaxy.  

From a broad Galactic perspective, astronomers have learned a great deal how habitable planets arise in the Galaxy:  gasses and dust particles ejected into the interstellar medium from red giant stars; the molecules and dust in molecular clouds; the gravitational collapse of cloud cores to form protostars with protoplanetary disks; the solid particles in the disks merge into protoplanets; the protoplanets may interact with the disk (e.g. migration to form hot Jupiters) and other protoplanets (e.g. eccentric Jupiters); the planetary systems then last for billions of years to be detected by exoplanetary surveys.  

Earth-size and Earth-mass planets are not rare in the galaxy!

With this foundation in empirical astronomical findings, we now turn to Theme V of the course where we ask more speculative questions: Could there be living organisms on these other planets?  Could such organisms be intelligent, even more advanced technologically than us?  Could such aliens have visited us, or could we visit them in the future?  Could there be a Galactic-wide civilization?
