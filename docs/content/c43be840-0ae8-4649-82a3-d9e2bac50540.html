Hundreds of the exoplanets -- from transit, radial velocity, microlensing and direct imaging surveys --  are in multiple planet systems.  Watch the totally fun animation of all known Kepler multiple planet systems below.  This is a winner!!  

In this class, we select a few of the fascinating exoplanetary systems that have been characterized in some detail.  

Upsilon Andromedae

This was the first exciting multiplanetary system, discovered during 1996-1999 with the radial velocity method.  Below is a diagram of the orbits (with Mercury, Venus and Earth shown with dashed orbits), and an artist's view of the planets.  This system illustrates two common phenomena not seen in our Solar System:  a "hot Jupiter" orbiting extremely close to the host star (ups And b, 4.6 day orbit); and orbital that are more eccentric than the near-circular orbits in our Solar System.  

HR 8799

Direct imaging of exoplanets in telescopes is extremely difficult as reflect such a tiny faction of the starlight and are so close to the star. But it has succeed in a few cases with technologically advanced active optics and coronographic techniques on large telescopes. The most famous case is HR 8799 in the image below. The bright central star has been suppressed and three planets are revealed. The arrows show their motion over several years, and estimates of their masses (around 10 Jupiter masses) and orbits (far beyond our Kuiper Belt) are shown.

Gleise 581

Gl 581 is a nearby star with a very complicated radial velocity behavior that can only be explained if the star is orbited by 4, or possibly more, planets.  Here is the early radial velocity data and an artists conception of the planets involved.  All four planets orbit very close to the host star, closer than Mercury orbits our Sun.  The blue region in the second diagram is the Habitable Zone that we will discuss next week.  

Kepler 11 bcdefg

This is one of the most spectacular planetary systems to emerge from NASA's Kepler satellite's monitoring of ~160,000 stars: 6 planets orbiting a Sun-like star.  The top diagram shows the data:  brightness (vertical axis showing the star's brightness measured with incredible accuracy of around 0.01%) plotted against time (horizontal axis, showing ~1.5 years of monitoring).  Dozens of tiny dips are seen in the star's brightness that are modeled as the transits of 6 planets (second and third panel below).  These planets are `super-Earths' and `Neptunes' with masses around 3-20 Earth masses.  All of them orbit close to the host star, too hot to be in the star's Habitable Zone. The planets' radii, measured from the ingress/egress times of the transit eclipses, are consistent with densities around 0.5-2 g/cm3.  This suggests they are made mostly of gases rather than solid rock.    

  

Note that the multiplanet models are not always reliable: the data is sparse or noisy, and different multiplanet models can often reasonably fit the same dataset.  The systems we see here are also probably incomplete: there easily can be more lower-mass (Earth-like??) planets in these systems that we can't yet detect.  Nonetheless, we are very confident that multiple planets are indeed present because, in some cases, we can detect very tiny gravitational interactions between the planets.  In Kepler data, the transits are seen sometimes to be slightly earlier or later than expected, consistent with gravitational tugs by the other planets, which can be detected as transit timing variations.
