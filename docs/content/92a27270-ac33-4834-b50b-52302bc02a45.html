As outlined in my video above, this week will have no lecture.  Rather the class will learn about, and through computer simulations, experience some scientific issues concerning possible effects of life forms on a planet's climate.  We will then discuss the issues with each other on a Discussion Forum on the ANGEL Web site.  (This is an asynchronous discussion: we can each login when convenient over the next week, and start new conversations or add to existing ones.)  You will be graded up to 5 points for your contributions to the ANGEL Discussion Forum.  Click on the "Syllabus" tab above to see the small alteration in the course grading (highlighted in yellow).

Please do the following:

  • Read about the very recent findings that Arctic Ice cap is melting faster than previously seen.  This is yet another warning that global warming induced by releast of carbon dioxide (and other greenhouse gases) is causing rapid changes in our climate.  News articles on this finding can be found here, here, here and here.
  • Read about DaisyWorld, a simulation of hypothetical planets with life forms that reflect (white daisies) and absorb (black daisies) sunlight to different degrees. DaisyWorld is discussed in our textbook `Extrasolar Planets and Astrobiology' by Scharf in sec 6.8 (pp. 280-295). As before, we are not responsible for the equations. Online background on DaisyWorld can be found on Wikipedia. Note the word `albedo' means `reflectivity': an object with albedo = 0 is black (fully absorbing of incident light) while an object with albedo = 1 is white (fully reflecting). 
  • Play with interactive DaisyWorld simulations.  There are two we can try:  the Northwestern University model and the DaisyBall model. In each case, read the introductory material  and click on the "Run" button. Start with some simple situations, such as all-white or all-black daisies. What happens? Then try a mixture of black and white daisies. Do you get an oscillating solution? Try to find a combination that gives a comfortably habitable planet; say temperatures in the range 10-30 degrees Centigrade. Is it easy to find one? Try other situations: a more or less luminous Sun, several daisy types, changing daisies, and so forth. Do you have any thoughts about the implications for advanced life forms on other planets?
