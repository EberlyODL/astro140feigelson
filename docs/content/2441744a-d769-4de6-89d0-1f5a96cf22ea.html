Important stages of evolution of life on Earth

  • Development of the first self-reproducing molecular systems, potentially based on RNA or another self-catalyzing information-carrying molecule.  A model of RNA world is outlined in sec 1.8.1.  
  • Development from a variety of primitive bacteria now seen in marshes, hot springs, caves and deep ocean vents. Probably ~3.8 Gyr ago.  Below are photos of ancient forms of life before photosynthesis arose that exist today around deep ocean vents (Fig 1.36) and in boiling hot springs in volcanic zones (Fig 2.27).
  • Development of photosynthetic bacteria and algae (e.g. cyanobacteria which may have made the stramatolites) producing the oxygen-rich atmosphere.  3.0-2.5 GYr ago
  • Development of a more complex biochemical system seen in most cells today (eukaryotes, mitochondria). 1.2 Gyr ago
  • Development of simple animals (Precambrian era, 0.6 Gyr ago) including fishes (0.4 Gyr ago), insects & dinosaurs (0.25 Gyr ago), mammals (0.065 Gyr ago), primates (0.005 Gyr ago), hominids (~0.003 Gyr ago) and the hypothetical most-recent common human ancestors to all humans today (~0.0002 Gyr ago).

Most species that have lived on Earth are now extinct (see Tree of life for many examples). The processes of the evolution of life, proposed in the 19th century by Charles Darwin, are well-established. In particular, recent projects to determine the sequence of the DNA genetic codes of different organisms shows without doubt that all life forms on Earth, including humans, are related to each other.  

Tree of LIfe

While there is no single classification system of living organisms that all biologists accept, most modern systems are roughly similar.  First prokaryotes originated ~3.8 GYr ago during Archean eon (3.9-2.5 GYr).  There were two types:

  • Archaebacteria  Developed in early atmosphere rich in CO2  and NH3. Present today as extremophiles of several types: some live in very hot water (thermophiles), acids or salty water that metabolize methane or sulfur.  Some are found in hot springs (e.g. Yellowstone National Park), dark caves, deep-sea vent environments, etc.  Some live within cracks of rocks or in ice. 
  • Eubacteria  Had early type of anerobic (no oxygen) photosynthesis. Spread over huge mats on seas, producing calcium-rich stromatolite fossils. After 3.0-2.5 GYr, some developed modern oxygen-producing photosynthesis; e.g. contemporary cyanobacteria.  Oxygen slowly accumulates in the atmosphere over the next 1-2 GYr.   Some symbiotically entered the early eukaryotic cells as chloroplasts (~1.0 GYr ago), giving rise to modern photosynthetic protists and plants.  Other eubacteria developed aerobic respiration and entered.
  • Eukaryotes as mitochondria (~1.2 GYr ago).  Eukaryotes, which have more complex cell structure with nuclei and other specialized organelles, started appearing ~0.9 GYr ago, and are divided into three kingdoms: plants, fungi and animals.

Historically, life forms were classified by their appearance, structure, life cycle, etc.  These schemes have been superceded by molecular evolutionary genetics, where similarities in DNA, RNA, etc. are quantitatively measured.  A major result is the recognition of the Archaea kingdom of primitive bacteria may be the oldest form of life.  Our text gives a Tree of Life, a `phylogenic tree', in Fig. 1.37.  Here is another simple version:

The oldest known fossilized organism (3.9 GYr ago)  resemble cyanobacteria which are still common today (e.g. the scum in your kitchen drain may be cyanobacteria).  Past cyanobacteria are likely responsible for some oil deposits.  Fossil microorganisms are identified by their microscopic morphology (ofter filamentary or globular), their organic carbon content (the original DNA,  etc. is long gone),  and particularly the high isotopic ratio of 13C/12C characteristics of photosynthetic processes (sec 2.4.5).  Their ages are determined by radioactive dating of the rock formations where they are found.

Processes of evolution

The basic concepts of evolution of populations were developed by Charles Darwin during the 1800s (Box 1.1), but have been refined by population & genetic studies during the past half century following the discovery of DNA/RNA.  An individual's genetic composition is determined by processes such as:  fertilization which combines genetic information from two parents; crossing over and other shuffling of chromosomal information during cell reproduction; and mutation where a small-scale genetic change is inherited by offspring.  A population's gene pool is further affected by: natural selection where different genetic manifestations have different influences on individuals'  survival in different environments, and genetic drift as populations change.  Diverse life forms arise as populations adapt to varying conditions. Extinctions occur when all individuals of a species die out; this may occur gradually or in discrete events triggered by external forces (asteroid impacts, rapid climatic change, volcanism, continental drift). Most species of life that have existed on Earth (~millions) are now extinct.  However, throughout evolution, the basic biochemical basis of inheritance (DNA <--> RNA <--> proteins, metabolism using ADP/ATP, etc.) has remained largely unchanged.

Example of Evolution:  Origin and Diversification of Birds on Isolated Islands

Charles Darwin, the originator of modern theory of evolution, derived many of his ideas by studying the variety of animals on the isolated Galapagos Islands off the Pacific coast of South America. His analysis was based mainly on the easily seen characteristics like the structure of bird beaks.  Darwin did not know the underlying mechanisms for genetic transmission and diversification; this did not emerge until the 1950's when James Watson and Francis Crick used X-ray crystallography taken by Rosalind Franklin to unravel the structure and genetic code of DNA molecules.  

 

Today, Darwin's basic idea has been superbly validated by studied of the genetic code.  Above are results from a 2011 statistical analysis of sequences of >20,000 DNA nucleic acid bases for Hawaiian birds (bottom) compared to common birds on large continents (middle and top).  The left-hand  `phylogenic tree' is based on mitochondrial DNA while the (very similar) right-hand tree is based on nuclear DNA.  The Hawaiian birds are found to be closely related to Asian finches, and only distantly related to other birds like the sparrow.  Combining mutation rates with other data, the biologists can trace the progression of the Asian finches from the western Hawaiian islands (~5 million years ago) to the eastern Big Island (<1 million years ago).  See a news report on these findings.  

Life of Earth is incredibly rich and complex: there have been millions of species adapting to an incredible range of conditions.   A few of these species have astonishing intelligence, including us humans.  Yet all organisms share the same biochemical machinery (DNA, proteins, cells, etc.) and have closely intertwined genetic codes.  Despite the diversity of species, life on Earth is cohesive ... we, our puppy, the tree, the fruit fly and nasty bacteria are all cousins.
