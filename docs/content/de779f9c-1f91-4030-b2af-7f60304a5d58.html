When a massive body  passes in front of a luminous object (like another star) as seen from Earth, then the background starlight can be focussed and amplified by the intervening body. This process in known as `microlensing', a type of `gravitational lensing' that was known by Albert Einstein a century ago. As the foreground star passes slowly in front of the background star, the brightness of the background star shows a slow rise and fall in brightness, typically lasting several weeks. But if the foreground star also has one or more planets in a fortuitous orbital location, then the planet also causes a lensing event, but much quicker. See the diagram below.

Perhaps the most remarkable system found so far using gravitational microlensing is named OGLE-2006-BLG-109.  Its weird brightness variations are modeled by planets very similar to our Solar System: a Jupiter-mass planet and a Saturn-mass planet at distances 2 and 4 AU.  Here is the brightness variation seen in 2006.  Once the event was triggered, telescopes around the world (New Zealand, Australia, Israel, Canary Islands, Chile, United States) observed the star nearly continuously for ~10 days.  This was essential to capture the complexity of the event, and thus model the planetary system.
