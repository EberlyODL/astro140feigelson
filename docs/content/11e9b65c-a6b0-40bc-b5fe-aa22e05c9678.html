Let us now look at some of the collective properties of planetary systems known today, keeping in mind that these are small and biased samples.  

Results from Radial Velocity Surveys

Radial velocity surveys have various limitations, but they can find planetary systems with reasonable reliability when the planet's gravitional pull is sufficiently strong;  that is, the planet's mass can't be too small and its orbital period can not too long.  In particular, expect the radial velocity surveys to undercount the lower mass planets ... they are particularly good at finding the more massive gaseous (Jupiter-like) planets.  Using Newton's version of Kepler's Third Law of Planetary Orbits, we can estimate the mass of these planets ... but we don't get their radius.  

The following diagrams have been extensively discussed as important summaries of the radial velocity surveys.  The first one plots the planetary mass in units of Jupiter's mass against the semi-major axis (i.e. radius for a circular orbit) in Astronomical Units.  We use logarithmic units because the range of the variables is very large; so the masses range from around 0.001 MJup (1e-3) to 100 MJup (1e+2).  The second diagram plots the orbit's eccentricity (where 0 is a circular orbit and 0.9 is highly elongated) against the semi-major axis. It is useful to remember where Earth lies on these plots: it has mass = 0.003 MJup, radius = 0.09 RJup, semi-major axis = 1.0 AU, and eccentricity near 0.0.

The first plot seems to show three distinct groups: 

  1. Starting on the left side, we see planets with roughly Jupiter's mass and very small orbits, a few percent of an AU.  These planets orbit their host stars in a few days, rather than in a few years as we seen in our Solar System.  51 Peg b, the first clearly detected planet (1995), is one of these planets.  These are nicknamed hot Jupiters, because they low so close to their host stars; indeed, in a few cases, we see spectroscopic evidence that the gases from the planet's atmosphere are being heated and flow away from the host star.  But we need to explain how these planets got their tiny orbits considering that Jupiters are expected to form in the outer parts of protoplanetary disks. 
  2. We then see a  group of planets with masses around 0.03 MJup ~ 10 MEarth with orbit sizes around 0.1 AU.  These were found relatively recently using the most accurate spectrographs; then are nicknamed super-Earths.  In most cases, they lie too close to the host star to be in the Habitable Zones, so they are hot ... in some cases, the surface may be molten rock!
  3. A considerable number of radial velocity planets have masses several times Jupiter and orbit sizes of several AU.  Clearly, our Jupiter is a rather modest planet among the gaseous giants orbiting other planets. 

The second diagram above shows that many planets have very eccentric (elliptical) orbits.   This was a big surprise!  In our Solar System, planet orbits are nearly circular (eccentricities mostly less than that 0.05).  In the early years around 1995-2000, we found mostly eccentric Jupiters, but today with more sensitive spectrographs, we see eccentric super-Earths too.  This raises a big question: how did these planets got high eccentricities considering that they formed in protoplanetary disks where the orbits should be nearly perfectly circular.

If we count the number of planets found with radial velocity surveys against the number of stars that were observed, we found that at least 10% of all Sun-like stars examined show radial velocity signatures of Jovian planets.  And there are probably many more stars with less massive planets that cannot be detected in this fashion.  But by this method alone, we predict that (for a trillion stars in our Galaxy) there will be tens-to-hundreds of billions of planets in our Galaxy.  Wow!   

A major lesson from both diagrams is that few systems resemble our Solar System.  The terrestrial planets (Mercury through Mars) would not be detectable using radial velocity methods, so our Solar System would show only two planets on these graphs:  Jupiter (1 MJup mass at 5 AU semi-major axis and 5% eccentricity), and Saturn (0.3 MJup at 10 A.U. and 6% eccentricity). It is not yet clear whether our Solar System has a rare configuration of planets, or whether better surveys will find many Solar System analogs.  

Results from Transiting Planets

The Kepler survey is, by far, the best source of transiting planets.  Kepler has detected over 8,000 probable planetary systems of which >1000 are `confirmed' with additional spectroscopic evidence.  But it is widely believed that >90% of the Kepler `probable' planets are real, and that many more planetary transits are hidden by brightness variations in the host star.  That is why you are participating in the PlanetHunters citizen science effor to find new transits within variable light curves!

The plot below compares the radii of Kepler planets (in units of RJup, Jupiter's radius) to their orbit size (in units of AU, the Earth-Sun distance).  Recall that the transit method can give the planet's radius (by timing the ingress/egress of the transit lightcurve) but not the planet's mass or eccentricity.  

  1. We clearly see again a clump of Jupiter-like planets (R~1-2 RJup) with very small orbits (a~0.02-0.06 AU).  These are the hot Jupiters that we saw in the radial velocity surveys above.
  2. Most of the planets have sizes between ~0.07 and ~0.4 RJup.  For comparison, in our Solar System Neptune has radius 0.35 RJup and Earth has radius 0.09 RJup.  So these exoplanets are often called Neptunes, super-Earths, and Earths.  Note however the tilt in the lower envelope of the Kepler planets in this diagram. This is an instrumental limitation: transits of very small planets orbiting far from the star produce transits too faint for the Kepler detectors to see.  Thus there are only a couple (out of thousands) of Kepler exoplanets that are Earth twins with radius ~0.09 RJup and orbit ~1 AU.  

The Kepler Team has performed a final re-analysis of its data, and the latest list of Kepler planetary candidates should be announced in 2006 (possibly during our semester).  Below is a histogram of planet radii shown by the Kepler Team at conferences from their earlier analysis.   The most common planets found are super-Earths and Neptunes, and Jupiters are much rarer.  Earths (and sub-Earths??) are probably much more numerous than found due to instrumental limitations.
