As of March 2018, the Extrasolar Planet Encyclopedia (http://exoplanet.eu) lists:

  • 746 planets orbiting 553 stars found using radial velocity techniques, including 134 multiple planet systems.  These are found in the way Mayor & Queloz discovered 51 Peg b in 1995; repeated measurement of Dopper shifts in the spectra of stars looking for tiny periodic motions caused by the gravitational pull of orbiting planets. 
  • 2789 `confirmed' planets in 2082 systems including 461 multiple planet systems, plus several thousand very probable additional cases, found using transit detection.  These are found in the way that Charbonneau discovered HD 209458 b in 2000; repeated measurement of the brightness of a star looking for tiny dips in the star's brightness due to the passage of a planet across its disk (a transit).  The great majority of these transiting planets were found using  NASA's Kepler mission, but others are found using ground-base telescopes or the French/Euro CoRoT satellite (originally designed to study stellar variability and only incidentally finding a few dozen planets).
  • 67 planets orbiting 65 stars found using gravitational microlensing detection including 2 multiple planet systems.  These are found by staring at millions of stars in the crowded plane of the Milky Way Galaxy waiting for the rare occurrence that one passes directly in front of another.  The background starlight is then greatly amplified for a brief period and, if the foreground star has an orbiting planet in the right place, a secondary amplification is seen.  
  • 92 planets orbiting 85 stars found by direct imaging including 5 multiple planet systems.  These are found by directly imaging nearby stars with instruments equiped with active optics so that very sharp images are obtained.  Only planets with very large orbits, typically beyond the orbit of Uranus, can be seen in this fashion.  In addition, in most cases, the central starlight is removed with a dark spot in front of the detector (this is called a coronograph).   

Each method has its own limitations in detecting planets:  some can only find the more massive planets (radial velocities, microlensing), or the larger planets with smaller orbits (transit), or planets with the largest orbits (direct imaging), or planets with orbits that are fortuitously (by chance) observed at favorable orientations (radial velocities, transit, microlensing).  Astronomers try very hard to understand the limitations of each survey in order to calculate the global properties of all planets from the samples available today.  

This follows a broad principle of the scientific method:  we are usually interested in global phenomena or processes in the natural world, not just the properties of the small sample in our laboratory or telescope.  Note that this principle doesn't work where there is only one object, such as understanding global warming on the Earth today or understanding the origins of our Universe.  But it does work when there are vast underlying populations, such as planets orbiting stars in our Galaxy.

I can also make some personal comments from watching this field for 20 years

  1. Most of the discoveries were made possible by improving the sensitivity of existing instruments, sometimes made enormously better, by clever engineering.  My respect for engineering teams, in universities & industry, U.S. and Europe, is really high ... they are often superb!  
  2. I personally didn't believe that gravitational lensing of planets would work.  But with dozens of discoveries, often very valuable because they don't depend on the planet being near or far from the star, it clearly has been a success. This is a case where my evaulation at the time was wrong .... can't even trust my own judgment! 
  3. The discovery of planets is going very well, and spectroscopists are finally developing instruments that can characterize the planets' atmospheric composition and extent.  But the astrophysicists who try to model the origin of planetary systems are having a tough time.  The best earlier predictions proved entirely wrong when NASA's Kepler mission results emerged.  The physical processes of planet formation are not just a calculation of Newton'g gravity, but involves the physics of rocks, ices, dust, pebbles, moons, ion-molecular chemistry, radioactive decay, ... ...  I think that the theorists will be pondering these issues throughout the 21st century, just as theorists figured out the physical origin and structure of stars between ~1880-1970.  

How many planets in the Galaxy?

It is difficult to estimate the total fraction of stars hosting planetary systems from radial velocity or Kepler data due to the complicated limitations of each survey.  Kepler has found ~8,000 likely planets from observations of ~190,000 stars for several years.  But the true fraction of stars with planets is certainly much greater than 8,000/190,000 ~4%, because only a small fraction of planets will show transits when viewed from a single direction.  Also many stars have variable brightness or other problem that hinders finding planetary transits.  The radial velocity results indicate at least 10% of stars have Jupiter-like planets, and probably many more.  A recent estimate from microlensing studies (which has less bias but much smaller samples) suggests that the fraction may be as high as 50%, as illustrated on the European Southern Observatory website.  

Today the best estimates are 50% to 100% of stars have planets.  If the stellar mass of our Galaxy is around 300 billion solar masses (estimated from the Galaxy's rotation curve) and if the typical star has mass around 0.3 Mo (estimated from the observed distribution of stellar masses), then there are about 1 trillion (1x1015) stars in the Galaxy.  If nearly all of them have planets, then there is nearly a trillion planets in the Galaxy.
