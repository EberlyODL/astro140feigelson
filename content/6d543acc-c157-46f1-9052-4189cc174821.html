The expanding Universe

In the 1920s, the astronomer Edwin Hubble (shown below left) and others  obtained spectra of these galaxies to measure their motions towards or away from us based on the Doppler shift. To everyone's amazement, nearly all galaxies showed a redshift indicating they are all moving away from us. Furthermore, the redshift is greater for more distant galaxies, following a linear relationship v = Ho d (Hubble's law). Hubble's original data is shown below right; today we have millions of galaxy redshifts measured, extending the diagram to fainter magnitudes (rightward) and faster velocities (upward).   The only reasonable interpretation for all galaxies moving away from us in a linear fashion is that space itself is expanding, much like the rubber surface on a balloon expands as it is inflated.  By the middle of the 20th century, nearly all astronomers acknowledged that we reside in an expanding universe.

A powerful explanation of the expanding universe actually preceded the observations.  A brilliant young scientist named Albert Einstein had made profound changes to Newtonian physics. First, in 1905, he showed that Newton's laws of motion do not hold when speeds approach the speed of light. He then struggled for a decade to understand gravity in this new context, emerging with the theory of General Relativity in 1915. Based on the view that massive bodies alter the geometry of 3-dimensional space-time, Einstein's General Relativity gives a full and accurate explanation of the gravitational attraction between bodies. The mathematics was quickly applied to the universe by others who found that the theory predicts that the Universe should not be static: it should either expand or contract following specific mathematical formulae. Remember that this is not a vague idea, but a rigorous mathematical derivation based on Einstein's General Relativity.

Below are predictions of the theory of General Relativity for different model parameter values.  The orange curve represents a `closed' Universe where the expansion stops and reverses to a contraction due to a high density of matter (Omega_m).  Curves above the green line represent an `open' Universe that expand forever.  Observations of the masses of galaxies (including the mysterious Dark Matter) indicate that our Universe has Omega_m around 0.3 ... so our Universe will expand forever.  But since 1999, very convincing evidence has been acquired that the universal expansion is actually accelerating, as in the red curve.  The cause is another mysterious component of the Universe (denoted Omega_V here) called Dark Energy, a repulsive force in space-time itself.  The nature of Dark Matter (which gives an attractive force) and Dark Energy (which gives a repulsive force) are not understood today, but we are convinced they exist.

The cosmic microwave background

If the universe is expanding, then the galaxies were closer together and the average density of matter was higher in the past. Extrapolating to earlier times, there must be some instant when the expansion began, after which the density of the universe was extremely high. High densities imply high temperatures for normal matter. When dense matter is hot it radiates light with a `blackbody' spectrum. So the expanding universe model predicts:

  • the universe was formed in a `Big Bang' at a single instant. With the latest measurement of Hubble's constant, H_o=70 km/s/Mpc and other parameters, we have measured that the Big Bang occurred about 13.8 billion years ago.

  • the early universe just after the Big Bang was filled with light and particles at extremely high temperatures

  • the Universe should be suffused with light from the Big Bang following a blackbody spectrum

In the 1950s, physicist George Gamow calculated that the light from the Big Bang should cool as the universe expands, and should be a few degrees above absolute zero today. In the mid-1960s, this cosmic light was discovered, by accident, by Arno Pensias and Robert Wilson. They were working for Bell Laboratories (the U.S. telephone company) to investigate why static was present on long-distance telephone calls that used microwave signals. They found that the entire sky was the source of the radiation.  The discovery of the CMB was a powerful confirmation of the Big Bang model for the expanding universe.

The CMB has been extensively studied since then, most recently with the Planck satellite telescope built by the European Space Agency.  Their image of the CMB, released in March 2013, across the entire sky (after removal of extraneous emission) is shown above.  It was found to have exactly a blackbody spectrum with T=2.735 K, about 3 degrees above absolute zero. Note the ripples (called `fluctuations') in the brightness, all at levels around a few parts in a million of the overall brightness.  These provide strong constraints on the model values for the Big Bang model. Below is a diagram of the composition of the Universe obtained from the best-fit Planck model.  Only 4% of the `stuff' in the universe is regular matter: molecules made up of atoms, themselves made up of protons, neutrons and electrons. 72% is the mysterious `Dark Energy' that is accelerating the universe's expansion, and 23% is comprised of the different mysterious Dark Matter that slows the expansion. Note that in our course on Life in the Universe, we are  concerned only with 4% of regular matter from which stars, planets and biospheres are made.

The birth of galaxies

Even though the Universe has been expanding since the Big Bang, dispersing the atoms and cooling the light, gravity is still present. On local scales, atoms attracted each other, forming early stars and then galaxies. We have little observational knowledge about this phase of the Universe's history, hence its nickname `the Dark Ages'. It lasted roughly from 1 million to 1 billion years after the Big Bang, or the first few percent of the Universe's current age. Astrophysicists argue that an early generation of stars formed during this time, quickly going supernova and polluting the gas with small amounts of elements heavier than helium. Detailed models show that gravitational attraction (involving both Dark Matter and regular matter) produced dense sheets and filaments that then collapsed to form galaxies and galaxy clusters. Big galaxies cannibalize smaller ones, and mergers of big galaxies are common. Massive black holes formed at the center of most galaxies at the same time. 

Astrophysicists have taking the known laws of physics (gravitational attraction, gas dynamics) with the conditions emerging from the Big Bang to produce computer simulations of the formation of galaxies as the Universe expands and cools. These simulations are among the largest scientific calculations ever made. See this excellent 4-minute YouTube video for a tour of the 2013 Illustris simulation, one of the most recent and comprehensive efforts.
