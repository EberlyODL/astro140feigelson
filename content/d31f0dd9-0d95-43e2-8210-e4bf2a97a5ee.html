Prior to the fly-by missions of the Voyager probes in the 1980s, there was little expectation of liquids needed for life anywhere in the Solar System beyond Mars. The reason is simply temperature: the Sun's light is so faint at these distances that no body's surface can reach the melting temperature of water.  Scientists expected that the moons would either be rocky like the asteroids, or icy with ancient frozen surfaces, showing only craters from many asteroid impacts.  Jupiter's biggest moon, Callisto (shown below), is a good example of this, as well as many smaller Jovian moons.  

It was thus a big surprise when Voyager found several moons with young, active surfaces, showing few impact craters. Understanding these moons was a major motivation for the complex and expensive missions of the 2000s: the NASA's Galileo probe, which orbited within Jupiter's system, and European Space Agency's (ESA's) Cassini mission that is still orbiting within Saturn's system.  Jupiter's innermost moon, Io (not shown here, see LUBS Fig 9.9), nearly entirely melted by tidal stresses, has many active volcanoes that perpetually cover the surface with new material.  

But it was Jupiter's moon Europa (and similarly, Ganymede) that seemed most relevant to the search for life in the Solar System. It has a water ice surface like Callisto, but very few craters.  The surface must have been melted and refrozen.  The strange grooves, rough patches, and multicolored ice suggest that this melting is a recurrent process, not from a single violent event. Study the pictures below.  Dark ice fills some grooves and patches on the surface.  This suggests that the ice covering is not very thick, and liquid (or slushy) water lies below and wells up when the surface cracks.  The rough patches on the surface (chaotic terrain) may be the product of ice plates grinding against each other, similar to mountain-building on Earth due to plate tectonics. 

This requires that Europa have a liquid water layer beneath a relatively thin ice crust.  This is supported by the presence of a weak magnetic field around Europa.  What keeps it above freezing temperature?  Perhaps it is tidal forces (weaker than Io's) due to its slightly elliptical orbit and perturbations by other moons.  Perhaps large impacts episodically melt the interior.  The thickness of the ice crust and the water ocean is quite uncertain; below are two models for Europa's interior.  NASA engineers and scientists have conceptually designed a mission that would land on Europa with a probe that would melt its way through the ice and swim in the hidden ocean.  But this ice-penetrating robot mission has not been approved by NASA or Congress.

  

 

Jupiter's moon Europa is not the only one that appears to have melted interior.  Neptune's moon Triton is bizarre, half covered with an ancient cratered surface and have with a smooth uncratered surface.  Perhaps it collided with another moon and partially melted.  Saturn's moon Enceladus shows some craters, but also has discolorations and grooves.  Here the Cassini probe has photographed geysers (essentially water volcanoes) along a long ridge.  As with Europa, this can be explained only if the surface ice is relatively thin and a (partially) melted layer lies below.  Here are some photos of Enceladus.

  

We finally come to Saturn's moon Titan, the largest in the Solar System.  It was already known from ground-based studies that it is the only moon with its own atmosphere, mostly nitrogen (N2) with some methane (CH4).  The surface cannot be seen in visible light due to the thick clouds, but Cassini has obtained detailed maps of the surface in infrared light and radar reflectance.   

 

The remarkable Cassini mission has a separate probe names Huyghens that was launched into Titan's atmosphere, descending slowly with parachutes, and landing softly.  The results were amazing!  The photos during the descent showed a complicated system of streams flowing into lakes.  The landing was squishy, onto a marshy surface.  The `rocks' on the surface (probably water ice) seemed embedded in a muddy material.  

What is the liquid on Titan?  It is much too cold for liquid water, and the atmosphere does not show water vapor.  The liquid seems to be a hydrocarbon: methane, perhaps combined with ethane (C2H6).  On Earth, this is called LNG, liquified natural gas, and is used as a fuel.  Titan seems to have a full climate with polar methane ice caps, methane clouds and rainstorms, rivers and lakes.  Cassini has traced a seasonal cycle involving warming with lake evaporation, and cooling with expanding ice caps.  Models of Titan's interior suggest that another liquid layer may be present, this time liquid ammonia (NH3). 

  

One can only speculate whether life can arise on Titan's surface where the temperature is around 90o K.  Recent laboratory experiments (similar to the Miller-Urey experiments for early Earth) indicate that amino acids and nucleic acids, critical components of proteins and DNA, could form if the temperature were hotter.  Astronomers note that, in a few billion years, the Sun will become a red giant star, boiling water on Earth but warming Titan to more habitable temperatures.  Altogether, after Earth, Titan seems to have the best chance for life in the Solar System with a solid surface, carbon-rich chemistry, a liquid solvent, and ecological variety.  Last year, NASA approved funds for preliminary study of a Titan Mare Explorer that would splash-land in one of its methane lakes and study its chemistry.      

We thus find that, contrary to any reasonable expectation before detailed study with robotic probes, that some Jovian moons have liquid on (or close to) their surfaces.  Europa and similar moons have water oceans below their ice coverings, and Titan has incredible liquid hydrocarbon lakes on its surface.

It is likely that, in coming decades, humans will send more probes to the moons to study them ... and look for primitive live forms.
