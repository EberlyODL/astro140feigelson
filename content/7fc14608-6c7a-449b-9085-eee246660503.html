Having established a reasonable model for how our Solar System emerged from its protoplanetary disk, we now see what changes are needed for exoplanetary systems.  Two topics are nicely understood: the origin of `hot Jupiters', and the origin of `eccentric Jupiters'.   The hot Jupiter issue is discussed in more detail in our text, An Introduction to Astrobiology, Chpt. 7.3.  Other issues are still under study and debate; for example, of all the gas giants undergo inward migration, then there should not be any terrestrial planets in the Habitable Zones! 

Planet Migration - Hot Jupiters (Chpt 7.3)

This was the most remarkable observational discovery, starting with 51 Peg in 1995.  Astrophysicists quickly found an explanation:  Jupiters form in the outer disks (around a few Astronomical Units where Jupiter and Saturn lie today), but can quickly "migrate" inward towards the star just after they formed in the protoplanetary disk.  This is due to complicated gravitational interactions between the protoplanet and the gas disk.  Calculations show that, as the protoplanet grows, it produces spiral-shaped ripples in the disk, migrates inwards towards the star, and opens a 'gap' in the disk,  Such gaps are detected in a number of protoplanetary disks.  

Look at several calculations of these effects by astrophysicists.  First, we have a brief animation by Frederic Masset of a Jupiter-like planet forming in a disk quickly (just a few hundred years) migrating inward and forming a disk 'gap'.   Here is another animation by Philip Armitage showing how the protoplanet is expected to form ripples in the disk and opens up the gap.  Masset has a pretty movie of how such a system might look if you visited it with a spaceship.  

Planet Interactions - Eccentric Jupiters, Reversing Migration

In our Solar System, the gas giant planets Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, & Neptune have nearly-circular orbits and lie in the same plane. But we have found that many of the exoplanetary systems discovered in Doppler (radial velocity) searches have Jupiters in high-eccentricity, inclined orbits. Fortunately, astrophysicists quickly found a convincing explanation for these eccentric Jupiters. 

When two or three proto-Jupiters form in a protoplanetary disk and start to migrate, their gravitational interactions can easily become resonant and unstable, as when one planet orbits at half the period of the other (we encountered this phenomenon earlier, with the four major moons of Jupiter). Unstable orbital interactions can easily lead to a cataclysmic realignment of the planets' orbits. From simulations of such systems, theorists find that a frequent outcome is for one of the gas giants to collide with the host star or be ejected from the planetary system; at the same time, the remaining gas giant acquires a high-eccentricity orbit. As most of the planetary systems found so far with Doppler searches have eccentric Jupiter-mass planets, this appears to be a common occurrence, with a relatively small minority of planetary systems having their Jupiters in nearly-circular orbits as in our Solar System. 

Planetary interactions also offer an escape from the relentless forces of planetary migration that tend to draw all young gas giant planets into the inner reaches of their planetary system, with severe consequences for any habitable-zone planets that might be forming there. Specifically, the gravitational interaction of two gas giant planets - for example, our Jupiter and Saturn - can act to halt or even reverse the inward migration of the inner planet (Jupiter, in our case), holding it in place until the protoplanetary disk has dissipated, and all of the protoplanetary objects are either ejected from the system, or incorporated into a planet. Once the disk is dissipated, the forces of planetary migration stop, and planetary orbits are generally stable for the very long term (> Ga). 

Growth and survival of terrestrial planets

The exoplanet discoveries have not given much insight yet into the phases of planet growth where planetesimals collide and merge to form protoplanets, and bombardment continues until most planetesimals are depleted or confined to stable asteroid belts.

The discovery of planet migration as a common process (supported by numerical simulations of protoplanetary disks) raises a big problem when gas giant planets formed in the outer reaches of the planetary system migrate inward: they will push the smaller rocky planetesimals and planets into the star or scatter them out of the system! So planetary systems with hot Jupiters are unlikely to host habitable terrestrial planets. A calculation by John Chambers (bottom animation here) suggests that the planets we see around older stars are just the remnants after many earlier planets spiraled into the star. A calculation by Sean Raymond shows a possible solution: here the Jovian planet migrates partway, allowing some small inner planets to form. As this movie progresses also watch the water shown in blue; water from icy planetisimals can impact the inner planets giving oceans on rocky planets near the Habitable Zone. It is still not clear why some Jupiters spiral in while others don't, and under what conditions inner rocky planets can survive.
