Goals

The goals of the paper assignments are as follows:  

  • Provide students with the opportunity to investigate a narrow scientific issue in some detail;
  • Challenge the student to assimilate diverse information into a coherent narrative;
  • Provide training in the art of scientific writing.

Format

The paper should be prepared with a word processing program like Microsoft Word or Google Docs, in the following format: 12pt Arial or Times Roman font; single-spaced, 1 inch margins; 8.5x11 inch or A4 paper. Papers may be submitted in either .docx or .pdf fomat with file names like "Lastname_Paper1.pdf".  For each paper, the text length is between 2 and 3 pages (roughly 7-11 paragraphs) and 1-2 additional pages with references (minimum three non-encyclopedia references) and useful figures or tables (e.g., jpg images imported from Web resources).  A cover sheet should be added with the paper title, student name and email, and date of submission.   The draft for paper #1 should be double-spaced (and hence will be 4-6 pages of text, plus references), so as to leave space for writing comments.  

Grading

Paper 1 will count 12% of the course grade, and Paper 2 will count 15%.  For Paper 1, students will submit a draft (a detailed outline with references would also be acceptable) to the instructor for improvement of writing and organization, but the grade will be based on the final version.  Half of the grade will be based on content: motivation, scientific details, and synthesis.  Half of the grade will be based on writing:  overall organization, well-designed paragraphs, sentences and grammar.

Paper Topic

For paper #1, paper topic should be chosen from the list below, or selected by mutual agreement with the instructor.  Generally speaking, your textbook and Wikipedia provide useful starting points for your research; however, they may not be your primary sources.   Consultation of a minimum of three additional high-quality resources is required. If you have difficulty identifying resources, please contact the instructor.  If you have trouble obtaining full text versions of important documents for the course, then please ask Penn State libraries for assistance.

Writing Style

Quality science writing is a skill well worth developing and exercising. The paper's overall organization looks like a U: it begins with a short statement of motivation or scientific issue; most of the text presents the evidence or argument (usually a combination of empirical findings and scientific model formulation); and the paper ends with a discussion of how the results relate to the original motivation. Within the text, the paragraphs should follow a logical trail. Within a paragraph, sentences should be well-constructed with informative nouns and verbs; use of the passive voice should be avoided as much as practical. No first person language (use of "i" or "We") is permitted.  For useful advice, please see:

  • Eric Feigelson's general advice for astronomy writing
  • Caty Pilachowski's tips for astronomy writing
  • William Safire's rules for writers
  • Writing in the sciences
  • Astro writing problems (Feigelson)

Regarding references, the common procedure in the physical sciences literature is to say in the text " ... blah blah (Smith 2010).  ... blah blah (Smith & Ford 2011) " and then in the references

Smith, A. B. 2010, A big result on extremophiles, http://www.blah.org   [or]

Smith, A. B. & Ford, E. B. 2011, A big result on extremophiles, Astrophysical Journal, 409, 525-530

Plagiarism is strictly prohibited. For example, you are not permitted to cut and paste text from the Web, rather than understanding and restating the issues in your own voice (with appropriate reference). Extensive quotations and quotations from any source are also inappropriate. Note that it will be much easier to phase an argument or conclusion in your own words if you have reviewed multiple references on the subject before writing your own text. The presence of plagiarized text may be checked using specialized software.  Plagiarism can have severe consequences.  If you have any doubts about whether something might be considered plagiarism, ASK BEFORE, not after you submit the assignment.
