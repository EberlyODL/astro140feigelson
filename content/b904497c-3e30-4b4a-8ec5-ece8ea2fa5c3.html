The images of the Milky Way Galaxy and other galaxies shown on the previous page were mostly made in visible light, the waveband where the surfaces of stars shine. Their temperatures are thousands of degrees Kelvin (above absolute zero). But the dark clouds where stars and planets form are much colder, only tens or hundreds of degrees Kelvin. These `shine' in the microwave and infrared bands of light, and in particular exhibit the emission lines and bands produced by particular molecules. 

The interstellar medium has two major components, gas and `dust'.  The gas is mostly hydrogen and helium (H and He) produced in the Big Bang, with elements like carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, silicon, iron  (C, O, N, Si, Fe) and other elements produced in red giants.  (We will discuss this next week.)  In the cold clouds, these atoms are in the form of molecules such as H2O (water), CO (carbon monoxide), CO2 (carbon dioxide), and dozens of other molecules.  The `interstellar dust' component is poorly named; it is mostly tiny powder-like particles of rock, such as SiO2 (silicon dioxide, called `quartz' when found in Earth rock).   There are also tiny solid particles of carbon resembling the black soot that comes out of combustion on Earth (e.g. a burning candle or log).

Interstellar molecules

To pursue our study of live in the universe, we first need to introduce some concepts and information about atoms, molecules and light. `Atoms' are tiny entities with a dense nucleus of protons and neutrons surrounded by a cloud of electrons. The number of protons defines the element: 1 = hydrogen (H), 2 = helium (He), …, 6 = carbon (C), 7 = nitrogen (N), 8 = oxygen (O), …, 26 = iron (Fe), and so on.  If you don't know or have forgotten the symbols for the elements, see a Periodic Table of Elements here.   A collection of atoms can be called a gas cloud.

When the cloud is hot, two atoms that collide will bounce off each other because the electron clouds (both negatively charges) repel each other. But when a cloud is cold and the collisions are slow and the two atoms can form a weak, but stable, bond. This occurs when the attraction of the protons is slightly stronger than the repulsion of the electrons. These entity of two or more atoms bonded together is called a `molecule'. `Chemistry' is the science devoted to the behavior and reactions between molecules.  Below are two diagrams of the water molecule, H2O.

The bonds between atoms in a molecule are not entirely fixed. They can stretch and bend in various ways (see fun moving diagrams). Even at very cold temperatures, the molecules can rotate. But the behavior of atoms and molecules is not like the behavior of large objects we experience in our daily lives. Large objects can move in a smooth fashion where any energy is possible. But tiny objects follow the rules of `quantum mechanics' and can be in only a fixed number of distinct energy states. A rotating or vibrating molecule can change its energy state by emitting or absorbing a particular wavelength of light. These wavelengths are generally not in the visible band, but have longer wavelengths in the infrared and microwave bands.

Thus, astronomers can observe the sky using specialized telescopes and look for emission or absorption lines at particular wavelengths associated with the quantum transitions of specific molecules. Even for simple molecules, these patterns of spectral lines can be very complicated, often blurring together to form messy bands. But they are unique for each molecule: just as a crime investigator can identify a person from a unique fingerprint, an astronomer can identify a molecule from its unique pattern of microwave and infrared emission lines. The study of the spectral lines and patterns produced by different molecules is called "molecular spectroscopy."

Here are infrared spectra for three common molecules: water, carbon dioxide, and ethanol (the alcohol in wine and beer). To show why this is relevant to our search for live in the universe, we show the infrared spectra of three familiar planets in our Solar System. We see that Venus, Earth and Mars all have carbon dioxide in their atmospheres, but only Earth has abundant water and oxygen (inferred from it chemical byproduct, O3 or ozone).

  

The result of several decades of molecular spectroscopy of interstellar clouds has shown an amazing variety of molecules in a gas that is mostly hydrogen (H2) and helium. Most of these molecules are organic; that is, they containing carbon. Some are quite simple molecules: carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), hydrogen cyanide (HCN). But others are more complex: ethane (C2H6), ether (CH3OCH3), ethanol (CH3CH2OH), acetone (CH3CH3CO), acetic acid (CH3COOH), a simple sugar (CH2OHCHO), antifreeze (HOCH2CH2OH), and controversially, possibly an amino acid (NH2CH2COOH). Some interstellar molecules are not organic (that is, without carbon): water (H2O), nitrogen (N2), ammonia (NH3), hydrochloric acid (HCl), salt (NaCl), sulfur dioxide (SO2), and so forth. Some molecules happen to have emission lines at wavelengths that are technically difficult to detect. This is the reason that the oxygen molecule (O2) was not discovered until 2011 using the new Herschel Space Telescope.

To discover the molecular material from which planets (and eventually living organisms) form, we have to study the sky with infrared and microwave telescopes. Below are photos of important telescopes at the three bands: the Gemini telescope on a Hawaiian mountaintop for visible wavelengths, the Spitzer Space Telescope orbiting the Earth at infrared wavelengths (two photos), and the ALMA (Atacama Large Millimeter Array) recently built on a high plateau in Chile.  This telescope cost over $1B as a collaboration of astronomers from North & South American, East Asian and European nations.
