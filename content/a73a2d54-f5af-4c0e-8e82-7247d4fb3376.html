When a planet orbits a star, the star orbits the planet!  Or, as Isaac Newton showed in his masterful work on the dynamics (motions and forces) of massive objects, both the planet and the star orbit the `center of mass' of the system.  Since the star is much more massive than the planet (our Sun is ~thousand times more massivethan Jupiter, and ~million time more than Earth), the star's motion is very small compared to the size of the planet's orbit.

If several planets are present, then they each pull the star in different directions at different orbital periods, giving rise to a complicated wobble pattern that would tell a good deal about the planetary system.  Here is how the Sun would look if its position could be accurately monitored from another star:

Unfortunately, a quantitative evaluation of this effect shows it is extremely small.  If the Sun were viewed from a star 10 parcsec distant and that is a very close star), then the maximum wobble is only about 1/1000 of an arcsecond.  Even the most accurate telescope available today cannot achieve this accuracy in the visible or infrared band.  It is there not a surprise that no planets have yet been discovered due to the  astrometric wobble of the host star.

This situation is expected to change in a few years with the arrival of data from the "GAIA" mission led by the European Space Agency (ESA). From space, beyond the distorting effects of Earth's atmosphere, the mission will be able to measure the positions of 1 billion stars in our Galaxy to micro-arcsecond precision, far beyond the capabilities of the best ground-based observatories. It is expected that GAIA will thus detect many massive planets via the astrometric motions of their host stars; however, its precision is not sufficient to promise detection of Earth-like planets in the habitable zones of (most of) these stars.
